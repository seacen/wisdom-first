# The taste: choosing the best wisdom for a need

This is the core of the skill. It contains **no list of domains and no list of
books** — on purpose. Domains can't be enumerated, and any such list would just
pre-load answers and rot. What travels across every field is a way of *judging*
which body of thought is the right one for the problem in front of you. Learn the
judgment; the specific frameworks and books then fall out of it on their own.

Hold everything against two anchors:

- **The live need** — what must this person be able to *do, decide, or feel* by the
  time we're done?
- **The scope** — how wide is the problem? A whole-life crossroads is a different
  size from a single-point skill.

Everything below is about choosing the wisdom that serves the live need, at the
right scope.

## The judgments

**1. Diagnose the live need — and notice it has two layers.**
The headline topic and the operative need are usually different. Beyond that, a
need has two layers, and people most often get stuck on the second while asking
about the first. There is the **cognitive** layer — what to say, what to decide,
how to structure it — and the **state-and-execution** layer — nerve, presence,
courage, composure, the ability to actually do the thing in the moment. When
someone signals fear, anxiety, freezing, "I can't bring myself to," "I don't know
how to start," or being overwhelmed, the live need is usually on the second layer,
even though the question looks like a content question. Solving only the cognitive
half and waving away the state half with a few reassurances is the single most
common misdiagnosis. Reach for the wisdom that trains the layer the person is
actually stuck on.

**2. Match the scope of the wisdom to the scope of the problem.**
A sprawling, whole-life or whole-situation problem ("I'm lost," "everything's a
mess," "where do I even start") needs a work whose scope is correspondingly broad
— a foundational, organizing system that addresses the *whole* of that territory.
A narrow, single-point problem needs a focused, specialist method. Reaching for a
narrow single-point tool when someone brings a whole-life problem is a scope
mismatch: it fixes a sliver and leaves the person still lost. Reaching for a grand
treatise when someone needs one concrete technique wastes their time. Size the
wisdom to the territory.

**3. Prefer the work that defines its field — the one you'd name if you could name
only one.** In most fields there is a work practitioners treat as *the* canonical
entry: broad enough to organize the territory, first-hand, and so established it
has become the shared language others build on and cite. Prefer it. It tends to
satisfy several other judgments at once — it is a real method from the source, it
is durable, and it is what lets the reader join the wider conversation and find
companions and follow-on reading. When such a defining work exists and fits the
need, it is almost always part of the right answer; reach past cleverer or more
obscure alternatives to make sure it is named. Among genuine equals, fame is a fair
tie-breaker — it usually means more shared vocabulary and more companion resources
— but it never trumps a better fit.

**4. Prefer a method you can practice over a description you can admire.**
The strongest work hands you concrete moves you could rehearse tonight and a
vocabulary to think in. Rank that above work that merely *observes* what good looks
like, *surveys* successful examples, *explains* why something matters, or *inspires*
without instructing. Insight that doesn't convert into a move is the wrong shelf for
someone with a task in front of them. When two works cover the same ground, the one
that turns the reader into a *practitioner* beats the one that turns them into an
*admirer*.

**5. Go to the source — but don't over-trace it.**
Prefer first-hand work by someone who forged and practices the method, over
downstream popularizers, journalists, or secondhand summaries that report it from
the outside and dilute it. But "source" means *the first-hand, field-defining
work* — not the earliest academic origin. When a first-hand work has itself become
the canonical method of its field, that *is* the source you want; do not burrow
past it to a more upstream but less complete or less usable ancestor just to seem
more original. The goal is the truest, most usable method — not the oldest citation.

**6. Prefer the durable model over the expiring tip.**
Favor work that installs a mental model the user will still be using in a decade
over a checklist that's stale within a year. The test: when the specifics of
today's problem are long gone, will this still be load-bearing? Durable wisdom is
built from first principles about how something fundamentally works, not from the
surface tactics of the moment.

**7. Distill to the moves that carry the weight.**
A good distillation is short and structured: the name (so they can find it), the
one-line essence, the few core moves the user can act on (spend your words here),
and one line connecting it to *their* need. If you're narrating a table of contents,
stop and ask: what are the three moves that matter for *this* problem? Say why
*this* source over its neighbors, and what depth the full read offers beyond your
summary.

**8. Offer a small, complementary set — usually two or three — then weave them.**
Don't hand over a single lonely pick, and don't dump a long reading list. The sweet
spot is **two or three** works chosen to cover *different facets* of the need:
typically one that frames and organizes the whole (the defining work for the
territory), one that addresses the layer the person is actually stuck on (often the
state-and-execution layer), and one sharper tool for the specific move at hand.
Each must earn its place by covering ground the others don't. Then, when you solve
the problem, **weave them into a single coherent approach** rather than applying
them side by side — the synthesis is where the skill shows. A well-chosen set also
widens the chance that the one work the user most needs is right there in front of
them.

## Two honesty clauses

**When the canon is thin.** If, after honest searching, no established body of
thought fits the need, *say so* and reason from first principles instead. A forced,
ill-fitting famous name is worse than an honest "there isn't a single classic that
nails this, so here is how I'd reason about it directly." Trust survives candor; it
does not survive a confident bad match.

**When you're unsure of a source.** You will usually know the canonical works cold.
When you are not certain of a precise title, author, or who originated an idea,
verify rather than guess, or hedge plainly. A confident wrong citation is more
damaging than an openly tentative one.

## A note on the optional author's shelf

The judgments above are the whole of the *general* selection skill, and they are
deliberately blind to any specific title. Separately, a skill's author may keep an
optional `references/authors-shelf.md` of a few hand-trusted favorites. That file is
**not** part of this reasoning — it is a personal taste layer, applied only as a
final tie-break among works this taste has already judged equally fitting (see the
note in SKILL.md). It exists so an author can pass their taste along to whoever
installs the skill; it never overrides fit, scope, or the judgments above.

## Why this stays general

These judgments name no field, so they apply to all of them — a hands-on craft, a
boardroom decision, a personal crossroads, a technical design, a private grief. In
every case the same questions decide the picks: *What does this person truly need,
and on which layer? How wide is the problem? Which work defines the territory, in
the practitioner's own hand, durably, as a usable method? And which two or three,
woven together, cover the need best?* Answer those honestly and the best frameworks
and books tend to select themselves — including the ones a casual observer would
never have reached for first.
