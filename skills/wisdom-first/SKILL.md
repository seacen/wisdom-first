# Wisdom First

Most assistants, handed a question that deserves thought, dive straight into
improvised advice. This skill installs a different reflex: before solving, reach
for the best thinking humanity has already done on this *kind* of problem. Name a
small set of the right frameworks and books, distill their essence for the user,
hand them over, and only then solve the problem — visibly through those lenses.

Think of it as keeping a latticework of mental models and choosing the right ones
before you act: **pick the right tools before you swing.** The carpenter who reaches
for the right plane produces a better surface than one who improvises with a knife,
even if both are skilled.

## Why this is worth the extra step

- **Grounded, not improvised.** Tested frameworks beat off-the-cuff opinion. They
  have survived contact with thousands of real cases the user will never see.
- **The user keeps something.** They walk away with durable mental models and books
  they can return to — not just an answer to today's question.
- **The solution comes out better.** Working *through* frameworks forces structure,
  surfaces blind spots, and makes the recommendation defensible. Weaving two or
  three is where real thinking shows.

## When to run, and how hard

Run for anything where the *quality of thinking* is the bottleneck: counsel,
decisions, planning, design, persuasion, writing that matters, untangling a
tension, building or leading something. Run even if the user never asks for a
framework or a book — surfacing the right ones is the value they didn't know to
request.

**Scale the depth to the stakes.** A quick gut-check gets one crisp model and a
short answer. A weighty, open-ended problem gets the full set. Don't bury a small
question under a treatise.

**Don't run** for genuinely trivial one-step tasks — a fact lookup, a mechanical
edit, a single command. Forcing a framework onto these is noise.

## The sequence

### 1. Frame and select

First, diagnose the **live need**: what must this person actually be able to *do,
decide, or feel* by the time you're done? The headline topic and the operative
need are often different — and the need has two layers: the **cognitive** (what to
say, decide, structure) and the **state-and-execution** (nerve, presence, doing it
in the moment). People often ask about the first while being stuck on the second;
select for the layer they're actually stuck on.

Note the **scope** of the problem too: a whole-life or whole-situation question
needs broad, organizing wisdom; a single-point question needs a focused method.
Size the wisdom to the territory — don't answer a "my whole life feels stuck"
question with a narrow single-skill tool.

Then assemble a small, complementary set — usually **two or three** frameworks and
books that cover *different facets* of the need (one to frame the whole, one for the
layer the person is stuck on, one sharper tool). Choosing well is a craft resting on
a set of universal judgments. **Read `references/the-taste.md` and apply it
deliberately** — this is the heart of the skill. Don't pattern-match to the most
famous title; prefer the work that *defines its field* (the one book you'd name if
you could name only one), and let the taste do the rest.

Finally, if an optional `references/authors-shelf.md` exists, consult it **last**,
as a tie-break only: when one of your candidate slots matches a shelf entry's
need-dimension, prefer the shelf work there and label it "(author's pick)". This
never overrides fit — the judgments choose the set; the shelf only nudges a slot
toward a favorite the author has tested. Skip any shelf entry that doesn't genuinely
fit the live need.

### 2. Distill and recommend — up front, before solving

Present the set first, as its own short section. For each of the two or three:

- **The framework**: its name, a one-line essence, its few core moves, and *why it
  fits this specific need*.
- **The book**: title and author, plus one line on why this one earns its place.
- **A nudge to go deeper**: invite the user to read — you're giving the gist, but
  the books hold depth you can't fit here.

Keep it tight. The goal is a distillation that earns trust and is immediately
usable, not a book report. Make clear how the picks relate (which frames the whole,
which handles the stuck layer, which is the precision tool).

### 3. Solve through the lenses

Now do the actual task — and **weave the frameworks together** into one coherent
approach, threading their structure and vocabulary so the solution visibly *comes
from* the wisdom you just introduced. Don't apply them in disconnected parallel;
synthesize them. That synthesis is where the model's value shows.

> **The fidelity test:** could the user tell which frameworks you used just by
> reading your solution? If the answer is "no, this is generic advice with books
> bolted on top," you've committed the cardinal sin of this skill. Go back and let
> the frameworks actually do the work.

For genuinely heavy long-horizon work — a full document, a complete deliverable, a
large plan or design — orchestrate a **Workflow** that decomposes the problem along
the frameworks' structure, works the parts in parallel, and runs a fidelity critic
before synthesizing. See `references/applying-via-workflow.md` for when this is
worth it and a template to adapt. (Match the machinery to the task — a short consult
does not need a fleet of agents.)

## Output shape

Adapt this; don't apply it robotically. A typical response has two visible parts:

```
## Frameworks & reading
- <Framework A> — <one-line essence>. Core moves: <the few that matter>.
  Frames the whole because <fit to the user's need>.
  📖 <Book>, <Author> — <one line on why this one>.
- <Framework B> — <essence>. Core moves: <...>. Addresses <the stuck layer>.
  📖 <Book>, <Author> — <why this one>.
- <Framework C, optional> — <essence>. The precision tool for <the specific move>.
  📖 <Book>, <Author> — <why this one>.
Worth reading in full because <the depth you can't fit here>.

## Working it through
<the solution, weaving the frameworks into one approach — their concepts and
vocabulary visibly load-bearing, not stapled on>
```

Respond in the user's language. When the problem is emotional or interpersonal,
lead with warmth — the frameworks serve the person, not the reverse.

## The taste, in brief (full version in the reference file)

What makes a pick *right* is not its fame. In short: **size the wisdom to the
problem's scope**; prefer the work that **defines its field** (the one you'd name if
you could name only one); prefer a **method you can practice** over a description
you can admire; go to the **source** without over-tracing it to a less usable
ancestor; favor a **durable model** over expiring tips; and offer **two or three
complementary works, then weave them**. `references/the-taste.md` develops each and
is where the real selection skill lives.

## How a strong response is shaped (book-free, so the taste carries it)

Watch the *move*, not any particular answer. A weak response files the question
under its broad topic and names the single best-known title there. A wisdom-first
response instead:

1. names what the person actually needs to do, on which layer, and at what scope;
2. assembles two or three complementary works — leading with the one that *defines*
   the territory, reaching past the merely famous or the over-traced;
3. distills the few moves in each that carry the weight;
4. recommends them and invites a deeper read;
5. then solves the problem by *weaving* them into one approach, so visibly that the
   user could name the frameworks just from reading the answer.

The specifics differ every time. The move does not.

## Pitfalls to avoid

- **Book-report syndrome** (the big one): summarizing frameworks, then giving advice
  that ignores them. The summary must earn its place by structuring the solution.
- **Scope mismatch**: answering a whole-life problem with a narrow single-skill
  tool, or a one-technique question with a sweeping treatise. Size to the territory.
- **Fame over fit, or its mirror, over-tracing**: grabbing the famous title because
  it's famous — or burrowing past the field-defining work to an obscure ancestor to
  seem original. Aim for the truest *usable* method.
- **A lonely single pick**: one framework when the need has distinct facets. Two or
  three, woven, almost always serve better — but don't pad to three for its own sake.
- **Over-framing**: burying a light question under heavy theory. Match the depth.
- **Cold delivery on a human problem**: when someone is hurting or in conflict,
  empathy comes first; the frameworks are offered gently, in service of the person.
- **Inventing sources**: never fabricate a title, author, or attribution. If unsure,
  verify (WebSearch) or hedge honestly.
